PHILIPPINE LAWS RELEVANT TO AFP FIELD OPERATIONS
==================================================
Reference: Republic Acts, Presidential Decrees, 1987 Philippine Constitution, AFP Judge Advocate General
Purpose: Field-level legal awareness for AFP personnel. Not a substitute for JAG counsel.

---

THE CONSTITUTION OF THE PHILIPPINES (1987)

ARTICLE II, SECTION 3 — Civilian Supremacy:
The civilian authority is, at all times, supreme over the military. The AFP is the protector of
the people and the State. Its goal is to secure the sovereignty of the State and the integrity
of the national territory.

AFP personnel serve the Constitution — not any administration, political party, or individual.
An order that violates the Constitution is NOT a lawful order and need not be followed.

ARTICLE III — BILL OF RIGHTS:
Applies to ALL persons in the Philippines, including suspects, detainees, and enemy combatants.
Key provisions relevant to field operations:
  - Section 2: No unreasonable searches and seizures. Arrests require warrant except in flagrante
    delicto (caught in the act), hot pursuit, or escaped detainee situations.
  - Section 12: Rights of persons under investigation (right to remain silent, to counsel).
  - Section 14: Presumption of innocence. Suspects are not convicted criminals.
  - Section 19: Prohibition against cruel, degrading, or inhuman punishment.

RA 9745 — ANTI-TORTURE ACT OF 2009

This is one of the most critical laws for AFP field personnel.

PROHIBITED ACTS (applies to ALL AFP personnel, in ALL circumstances):
  Physical torture:
    - Systematic beating, hitting, punching, kicking.
    - Food and water deprivation; sleep deprivation.
    - Electric shock; burning; freezing; suffocation or near-drowning.
    - Exposure to extreme temperatures.

  Psychological torture:
    - Threats to harm detainee or their family.
    - Mock executions.
    - Sensory deprivation (blindfolding + isolation for extended periods).
    - Humiliation, degradation, forced nudity.
    - Being kept in a "safe house" without notification to family or counsel.

PENALTIES:
    - Reclusion temporal (12-20 years imprisonment) for acts of torture.
    - Perpetual disqualification from public office.
    - Commanding officers who knew and failed to act face the same penalties.

KEY PRINCIPLE: Torture NEVER produces reliable intelligence. It produces confessions.
Under RA 9745, any confession obtained through torture is inadmissible in court.
Torturing a suspect destroys the case against them.

RA 7438 — RIGHTS OF PERSONS ARRESTED, DETAINED, OR UNDER CUSTODIAL INVESTIGATION (1992)

Applies the moment any person is taken into custody by AFP personnel.

MANDATORY RIGHTS TO BE INFORMED (in a language they understand):
  - The right to remain silent.
  - The right to have competent and independent counsel, preferably of their own choice.
    If they cannot afford counsel, one must be provided at NO COST.
  - That any statement they make may be used against them in court.

ADDITIONAL RIGHTS:
  - Right to communicate with their family or counsel immediately.
  - Right to be visited by or consult with a social worker and their priest/minister.
  - Right to medical examination before and after interrogation.
  - Right not to be subjected to any physical, psychological, or mental torture.

WAIVER OF RIGHTS:
  A detainee may waive their right to remain silent or to counsel, BUT:
    - The waiver must be voluntary, knowing, and intelligent.
    - The waiver must be in WRITING.
    - The waiver must be made IN THE PRESENCE OF COUNSEL.
  A waiver made without counsel present is legally invalid.

PENALTY FOR VIOLATION: AFP personnel who violate RA 7438 face criminal charges.
Any statement or confession obtained in violation of RA 7438 is INADMISSIBLE IN COURT.

RA 11479 — ANTI-TERRORISM ACT OF 2020

Relevant when operating against designated terrorist organizations (e.g., ASG, BIFF, NPA when
classified as terrorist organization by the ATC).

WARRANTLESS ARREST under ATA:
  - Authorized in flagrante delicto (caught committing a terrorist act).
  - In hot pursuit of a fleeing terrorism suspect.
  - ATC-authorized warrantless arrest may be extended (check current implementing rules).

DETENTION WITHOUT WARRANT:
  - Suspects may be detained for up to 14 days under ATA (extendable by 10 days).
  - HOWEVER: Detention must be reported to the Commission on Human Rights (CHR) within 48 hours.
  - Detainee retains all rights under RA 7438 even under ATA.

IMPORTANT: ATA designation requires ATC (Anti-Terrorism Council) authority. Individual AFP
commanders cannot unilaterally designate groups as terrorist. Follow orders from higher command.

RA 9165 — COMPREHENSIVE DANGEROUS DRUGS ACT OF 2002

Critical for any operation where illegal drugs are seized.

CHAIN OF CUSTODY RULE (Section 21):
  When drugs are seized, the following must happen IMMEDIATELY AT THE SCENE OF SEIZURE:
    1. Physically inventory all seized drugs in the presence of:
         a. The accused or their representative/counsel
         b. An elected public official (barangay captain, councilor, or higher)
         c. A representative from the National Prosecution Service (DOJ) OR media representative
    2. Photograph the drugs, paraphernalia, and documents at the seizure site.
    3. Mark each item immediately upon seizure.
    4. Prepare an inventory receipt signed by all witnesses present.

  FAILURE TO FOLLOW CHAIN OF CUSTODY = case dismissal. Courts have acquitted drug suspects
  solely because AFP/PNP failed to follow Section 21 correctly.

FIELD GUIDANCE:
  - Always request a barangay official to witness drug seizures before moving the evidence.
  - If witnesses are not immediately available, document WHY and what justifiable grounds exist.
  - Turn over seized drugs to the nearest PNP Crime Laboratory within 24 hours.

RA 10121 — PHILIPPINE DISASTER RISK REDUCTION AND MANAGEMENT ACT (2010)

AFP mandated role in disaster response:

  - AFP units may act to save lives WITHOUT waiting for civilian orders when immediate action
    is required to prevent loss of life.
  - AFP operates under coordination with NDRRMC (National Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Council)
    and local DRRM offices during declared calamity.
  - AFP resources (aircraft, vessels, personnel) may be tasked by NDRRMC for disaster relief.

AUTHORITY TO ACT: When there is a declared state of calamity, AFP field commanders may:
  - Conduct search and rescue without individual mission orders.
  - Commandeer transport, equipment, and supplies as necessary (with documentation and receipts).
  - Establish temporary evacuation centers.
  - Coordinate directly with LGUs, DSWD, and DOH without routing through HQ.

DOCUMENTATION: Even in disaster response, document all actions, assets used, and resources
distributed. This protects personnel from later accountability issues.

RA 10591 — COMPREHENSIVE FIREARMS AND AMMUNITION REGULATION ACT (2013)

Relevant when AFP discovers illegal firearms during operations:

  - AFP personnel carry weapons under military authority — this law governs CIVILIAN firearms.
  - Unlicensed civilian firearms found during operations: seize, document, and turn over to PNP.
  - Do not allow seized civilian firearms to be added to AFP inventory or used operationally.
  - Issue a receipt for any seized firearm. Maintain chain of custody documentation.

LOOSE FIREARMS in conflict areas:
  - Encountered illegal firearms must be inventoried and submitted through proper channels.
  - Improper handling of seized weapons can result in criminal liability for AFP personnel.

PD 1829 — OBSTRUCTION OF JUSTICE

Applies to civilians (and potentially others) who:
  - Harbor, conceal, or assist any person AFP is pursuing.
  - Destroy evidence of a crime.
  - Warn suspects of an impending operation.
  - Give false information to mislead AFP operations.

FIELD APPLICATION:
  - Documenting obstruction creates legal grounds for arrest.
  - Individuals who harbor armed groups can be charged under PD 1829 and potentially as accessories
    under the Revised Penal Code.
  - Coordinate with PNP for arrest authority in civilian obstruction cases.

RA 7077 — CITIZEN ARMED FORCES OF THE PHILIPPINES RESERVIST ACT

Defines the legal basis for CAFGU (Citizen Armed Force Geographical Unit) operations.

  - CAFGU members operate under AFP command and authority.
  - CAFGU members are subject to the same ROE, legal obligations, and standards as regular AFP.
  - AFP officers are responsible for the conduct of CAFGU units under their command.
  - Human rights violations by CAFGU = command responsibility of the AFP officer in charge.

---

COMMAND RESPONSIBILITY

Under Philippine law and international law, commanding officers may be held criminally liable for
acts of their subordinates if:

  1. The commander KNEW or SHOULD HAVE KNOWN that violations were occurring, AND
  2. The commander FAILED to take necessary and reasonable measures to prevent or stop them.

This applies to:
  - Human rights violations (torture, extrajudicial killing, forced disappearance).
  - Illegal detention.
  - Pillage (looting) of civilian property.
  - Sexual violence by troops.

PROTECTION: Commanders who ACTIVELY prevent violations and report them through proper channels
are protected. Commanders who cover up violations face the same liability as the perpetrators.

EXTRAJUDICIAL KILLING AND ENFORCED DISAPPEARANCE

RA 9851 — Philippine Act on Crimes Against International Humanitarian Law (2009):
  - Extrajudicial killing of civilians or POWs = war crime.
  - Enforced disappearance (arrest without acknowledgment) = crime against humanity.
  - These crimes have NO statute of limitations.
  - Perpetrators can be prosecuted before Philippine courts AND international tribunals.

A "salvaging" (summary execution) order from a superior officer is NOT a lawful order.
You are not obligated to follow it. You are obligated to report it.

---

LEGAL PROTECTIONS FOR AFP PERSONNEL

  - Soldiers acting in good faith, within ROE, under lawful orders are protected from civil and
    criminal liability for acts done in the performance of military duty.
  - Good faith requires: following ROE, respecting Philippine law, and treating all persons humanely.
  - AFP personnel facing civil or criminal charges arising from official duties may request
    legal representation from the AFP Judge Advocate General (JAG).
  - Report legal concerns immediately to your commanding officer and JAG representative.
