AFP FIELD MEDICAL PROCEDURES
=============================
Reference: AFP Medical Center Field Manual, TCCC (Tactical Combat Casualty Care) Guidelines
For use by AFP field personnel pending MEDEVAC or trained medic arrival.

IMPORTANT: These procedures are for emergencies when no medic is available.
Always defer to a trained Combat Medic (18D/MOS 68W equivalent) when present.
Call for MEDEVAC as early as possible — do not delay the call to treat first.

---

TACTICAL FIELD CARE PRIORITIES (MARCH)

M — Massive Hemorrhage (control life-threatening bleeding first)
A — Airway (ensure airway is open)
R — Respiration (treat chest wounds, pneumothorax)
C — Circulation (treat shock, IV fluids if available)
H — Hypothermia/Head injury (keep warm, monitor consciousness)

---

MASSIVE HEMORRHAGE — TOURNIQUET APPLICATION

Indications: Severe bleeding from a limb that cannot be controlled by direct pressure.

Procedure:
  1. Apply tourniquet 2-3 inches ABOVE the wound (not over a joint).
  2. Pull strap tight — tighten the windlass rod until bleeding STOPS completely.
  3. Lock the windlass. Secure the rod so it does not unwind.
  4. Write the TIME of application on the patient's forehead or tourniquet tag with marker.
     Example: "TQ 1435H"
  5. Do NOT remove the tourniquet in the field. Hospital decides when to remove.
  6. If bleeding continues through tourniquet: apply a SECOND tourniquet immediately above the first.

Preferred: CAT (Combat Application Tourniquet) or SOFT-T Wide.
Improvised: belt, cloth strip, or bootlace with a stick as windlass.
A tourniquet that is too loose will cause MORE bleeding. Tighten until pulse below tourniquet is gone.

---

WOUND PACKING (Junctional / Groin / Armpit Wounds)

Tourniquets cannot be used on wounds to the neck, groin, armpit, or torso.

Procedure:
  1. Pack wound tightly with gauze (hemostatic gauze preferred: Combat Gauze / QuikClot).
  2. Press the gauze deep into the wound cavity with your fingers or thumb.
  3. Apply firm, continuous direct pressure for a MINIMUM of 3 minutes.
  4. Do not remove packing once applied.
  5. Apply pressure bandage over the packed wound to maintain compression.

---

AIRWAY MANAGEMENT

Unconscious patient — check airway first:
  1. Tilt head back, lift chin (if no suspected spinal injury).
  2. If spinal injury suspected: jaw thrust only — do not tilt head.
  3. Look in mouth. Remove visible debris, vomit, blood with fingers or suction.
  4. Roll patient into recovery position (on side) to prevent aspiration if unconscious and breathing.

Nasopharyngeal Airway (NPA) — if available and trained:
  - Lubricate and insert through nostril, bevel toward septum.
  - Do not use if patient has obvious skull fracture or severe facial trauma.

Signs of airway obstruction: gurgling, snoring, stridor, no breath sounds, blue lips.

---

CHEST WOUNDS (OPEN/SUCKING)

Signs: bubbling or sucking sound from chest wound, breathing difficulty, frothy blood.

Procedure:
  1. Seal the wound immediately with a chest seal (vented preferred) or improvised occlusive dressing.
  2. Improvised: plastic bag, MRE wrapper, or any non-porous material taped on 3 sides
     (leave one side open to allow air to escape — vented seal).
  3. Position patient sitting upright or slightly inclined if possible.
  4. Monitor breathing continuously.

TENSION PNEUMOTHORAX — signs: severe breathing difficulty, trachea shifted to one side,
absent breath sounds on one side, neck veins bulging, patient deteriorating rapidly.
This is IMMEDIATELY LIFE-THREATENING. Trained medic required for needle decompression.
If no medic: lift one corner of the chest seal to release trapped air, then reseal.

---

GUNSHOT WOUNDS — GENERAL

  - Do NOT remove objects embedded in a wound (knife, shrapnel, bullet fragments).
    Embedded objects may be plugging blood vessels. Removal causes massive bleeding.
  - Cover penetrating object with bulky dressing, stabilize with improvised ring pad.
  - Wounds to abdomen: cover with wet dressing. Do not push protruding organs back in.
  - Do not give food or water to patients with abdominal wounds.

---

BURNS

Classification:
  - 1st degree: redness only (sunburn). Treat with cool water.
  - 2nd degree: blisters form. Cover with clean dressing. Do not pop blisters.
  - 3rd degree: skin is white, leathery, or charred. Patient may feel no pain (nerve damage).
    Do not remove clothing stuck to burn. Cover loosely. MEDEVAC immediately.

Treatment:
  1. Remove from heat source. Extinguish flames (stop-drop-roll, smother with cloth).
  2. Remove jewelry, watches, belts near burn BEFORE swelling starts.
  3. Cool with clean water for 10-20 minutes (not ice — ice causes further tissue damage).
  4. Cover with clean, non-fluffy dressing. Do not use cotton wool directly on burn.
  5. Treat for shock (lay flat, elevate legs if no chest/head injury, keep warm).
  6. Burns over 10% of body surface area = MEDEVAC required.

---

SNAKE BITE

Philippine cobras, sea snakes, and pit vipers are found in AFP operational areas.
Philippine cobra venom causes rapid respiratory failure — act fast.

DO:
  - Immobilize the victim completely. Keep the bitten limb BELOW heart level.
  - Remove rings, watches, and tight clothing near the bite before swelling begins.
  - Note exact time of bite and describe the snake (color, size, markings).
  - Request MEDEVAC immediately.
  - Keep patient calm and still — movement spreads venom faster.

DO NOT:
  - Cut the bite wound.
  - Suck out the venom.
  - Apply a tourniquet or tight bandage.
  - Apply ice, heat, or electric shock.
  - Give alcohol, aspirin, or ibuprofen.
  - Allow the patient to walk if avoidable.

Monitor: breathing difficulty, drooping eyelids, excessive saliva = cobra envenomation.
This is a medical emergency. Antivenom must be administered at a hospital.

HEAT INJURIES

HEAT CRAMPS: muscle cramps during exertion. Rest in shade. Drink water with salt (oral rehydration salts).

HEAT EXHAUSTION:
  - Signs: heavy sweating, pale/moist skin, weakness, dizziness, nausea, headache.
  - Treatment: move to shade, remove excess clothing, apply cool wet cloths, give water slowly,
    elevate legs. Rest 30 minutes minimum. Monitor for progression to heat stroke.

HEAT STROKE (LIFE-THREATENING):
  - Signs: HOT DRY skin (sweating has stopped), confusion, slurred speech, seizures,
    loss of consciousness. Temperature above 40°C if thermometer available.
  - Treatment:
    1. MEDEVAC immediately — this is a medical emergency.
    2. Remove to shade. Remove clothing.
    3. Wet entire body and fan aggressively — goal is maximum evaporative cooling.
    4. Apply ice packs to neck, armpits, and groin if available.
    5. Do not give water if patient is confused or unconscious (aspiration risk).
  - Do not delay cooling while waiting for MEDEVAC.

---

DROWNING / NEAR-DROWNING

  1. Remove from water. Ensure scene is safe before entering water.
  2. If unresponsive and not breathing: begin CPR immediately.
  3. Do not waste time trying to drain water from lungs — begin CPR.
  4. CPR: 30 chest compressions (hard and fast) followed by 2 rescue breaths. Repeat.
  5. Continue CPR until patient recovers, trained help arrives, or physically impossible to continue.
  6. All near-drowning patients need hospital evaluation even if they recover — secondary drowning risk.

---

SHOCK

Signs: pale/cold/clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, rapid breathing, confusion, thirst.

Treatment:
  1. Control all bleeding (tourniquet, wound packing).
  2. Lay patient flat. Elevate legs 15-30cm UNLESS: head injury, spinal injury, or breathing difficulty.
  3. Keep patient warm — cover with blanket or poncho. Hypothermia worsens shock.
  4. Do not give food or water by mouth if unconscious or has abdominal wound.
  5. MEDEVAC immediately.

---

9-LINE MEDEVAC REQUEST FORMAT

Transmit this to HQ/aviation as soon as casualties occur. Don't wait until treatment is complete.

LINE 1: Location of pickup site (grid coordinates or landmark)
LINE 2: Radio frequency and call sign at pickup site
LINE 3: Number of patients by precedence:
         U = Urgent (life-threatening, within 1 hour)
         P = Priority (serious, within 4 hours)
         R = Routine (stable, within 24 hours)
LINE 4: Special equipment needed (hoist, extraction, none)
LINE 5: Number of patients by type:
         L = Litter (cannot walk)
         A = Ambulatory (can walk)
LINE 6: Security of pickup site (no enemy, possible enemy, enemy in area, enemy escort required)
LINE 7: Method of marking LZ (smoke color, panels, lights, none)
LINE 8: Patient nationality and status (AFP military, civilian Filipino, foreign national, EPW)
LINE 9: NBC contamination (nuclear, biological, chemical — state type or "None")

Example:
"MEDEVAC, MEDEVAC, MEDEVAC — this is KILO-6.
Line 1: Grid 456-789, 200 meters south of Barangay Sta. Cruz.
Line 2: Frequency 35.45, call sign KILO-6.
Line 3: 1 Urgent, 2 Routine.
Line 4: None.
Line 5: 1 Litter, 2 Ambulatory.
Line 6: Possible enemy.
Line 7: Green smoke.
Line 8: AFP military.
Line 9: None."

FIELD HYGIENE AND DISEASE PREVENTION

- Purify all drinking water (boil 1 minute, or use iodine/chlorine tablets).
- Malaria prophylaxis: take as prescribed. Do not skip doses in endemic areas (Palawan, Mindanao).
- Leptospirosis risk in floodwater: avoid wading without protection. Cover all open wounds.
- Dengue: use repellent, wear long sleeves at dawn and dusk. Report fever with rash to medic.
- Foot care: change socks daily. Dry feet. Report blisters before they become infected wounds.
- Diarrhea: oral rehydration salts (ORS) first. Report bloody stool, high fever, or no improvement in 24 hours.
